REPLIT PROMPT — MURPHY METHOD “NEXT STEPS” PAGES (NO CODE)

You are building a “Next Steps” experience for users who have already completed the Murphy Method obstructive sleep apnea assessment and have already seen their results.

This content appears after the assessment and does not modify the assessment itself.

GLOBAL RULES

Do NOT change assessment logic or scoring.

Do NOT diagnose or prescribe.

Tone must be physician-led, calm, authoritative, and reassuring.

Audience: educated patient / smart consumer.

Emphasize that obstructive sleep apnea has multiple root causes, which is why treatment must be personalized.

Reinforce that this is a decision-support and education system, not a single test or device.

Encourage longitudinal reassessment after interventions.

Each pathway must have its own dedicated section and include the following headers:

What Your Results Suggest

Why This Matters for Your Health

What Usually Works Best for People in This Category

Your Murphy Method Next Steps

Clear Call-to-Action

Business model principles to embed across all pathways:

Free education builds clarity and trust

Paid guidance helps users avoid trial-and-error

Higher-risk pathways emphasize appropriate medical escalation

Lower-risk pathways emphasize conservative management and monitoring

PATHWAY A — OSA WITH MEDICAL PROBLEMS

Trigger: Elevated OSA risk plus medical conditions (heart disease, diabetes, hypertension, stroke history)

Key Focus: Urgent diagnostic priority due to comorbidities

Explain that:

Sleep apnea carries higher risk when combined with medical disease

Symptoms may underestimate danger

Timely diagnosis matters

Unique Next Steps:

Formal sleep study referral

Coordination with primary care and cardiology

Managing underlying medical conditions alongside sleep apnea

CTA:
“Prioritize Medical Evaluation”

PATHWAY B — OSA WITH OBESITY

Trigger: BMI ≥ 30 and/or neck anatomy score ≥ 2 with elevated OSA risk

Key Focus: Device-based airway treatment plus weight management

Explain that:

Weight and neck anatomy increase airway collapse

Treating sleep apnea improves energy and metabolic health

Weight loss alone rarely resolves apnea

Unique Next Steps:

CPAP trial

Oral appliance evaluation

Structured weight-loss programs

Bariatric surgery discussion when appropriate

CTA:
“Start the Airway + Weight Strategy”

PATHWAY C — OSA WITH NASAL PROBLEM

Trigger: Elevated OSA risk with nose zone score ≥ 2

Key Focus: Nasal obstruction as a major contributor

Explain that:

The nose plays a critical role in airflow and sleep quality

Nasal blockage worsens snoring, CPAP tolerance, and apnea severity

Nasal problems are often under-addressed

Unique Next Steps:

ENT nasal evaluation

Medical therapy (sprays, allergy management)

Nasal strips or dilators

Surgical options when appropriate

CTA:
“Optimize Nasal Breathing”

PATHWAY C2 — OSA WITH MANDIBLE & TONGUE OBSTRUCTION

Trigger: BMI < 30, elevated OSA risk, mandible zone ≥ 2 only

Key Focus: Jaw and tongue positioning

Explain that:

Lean patients can still have significant OSA

Tongue and jaw collapse can block the airway

CPAP is not always the best first option

Unique Next Steps:

Sleep dentist evaluation

Oral appliance therapy

Myofunctional therapy and tongue exercises

Follow-up sleep testing

CTA:
“Address Jaw & Tongue Factors”

PATHWAY C3 — OSA WITH MULTI-LEVEL OBSTRUCTION

Trigger: Elevated OSA risk with 2 or more non-nasal anatomy zones ≥ 2

Key Focus: Multi-level airway involvement

Explain that:

No single treatment works alone

Combination therapy is often required

Trial-and-error leads to frustration

Unique Next Steps:

Comprehensive ENT airway evaluation

Combination therapy planning

Staged surgical approaches when appropriate

CTA:
“Build a Multi-Level Treatment Plan”

PATHWAY D — OSA WITH PHYSIOLOGY PROBLEM

Trigger: Elevated OSA risk with low anatomy scores but PALM physiology flags ≥ 2

Key Focus: Non-anatomical causes (brain signals, arousal threshold, respiratory control)

Explain that:

The brain’s control of breathing matters

Anatomy may appear normal

This is a real biological form of sleep apnea

Unique Next Steps:

Advanced sleep study interpretation

Adaptive PAP strategies

Medication considerations

Neurology or specialty consultation

CTA:
“Address Sleep Apnea Physiology”

PATHWAY E — OSA WITH INSOMNIA (COMISA)

Trigger: Elevated OSA risk with moderate-to-severe insomnia and arousal threshold ≥ 2

Key Focus: Treat insomnia first or alongside apnea

Explain that:

Insomnia reduces apnea treatment success

Treating apnea alone often fails

Integrated care improves outcomes

Unique Next Steps:

CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)

Sleep hygiene optimization

Integrated COMISA treatment plan

CTA:
“Treat Insomnia and Apnea Together”

PATHWAY F — LOW RISK OSA / SNORING

Trigger: Low OSA risk with or without mild anatomy involvement

Key Focus: Conservative management and monitoring

Explain that:

Snoring does not always equal sleep apnea

Lifestyle factors often dominate

Monitoring is appropriate

Unique Next Steps:

Positional therapy

Alcohol avoidance

Weight management

Nasal strips

Sleep schedule optimization

CTA:
“Optimize Sleep & Snoring”

PATHWAY G — OSA WITH COMPLEX / MIXED FACTORS

Trigger: Does not fit cleanly into other pathways

Key Focus: Comprehensive, multidisciplinary evaluation

Explain that:

Many patients have mixed contributors

Complexity does not mean poor outcomes

Coordination is essential

Unique Next Steps:

Full sleep specialist workup

ENT + sleep medicine collaboration

Personalized treatment roadmap

CTA:
“Get a Personalized Sleep Strategy”

FINAL INSTRUCTION

Present all pathways clearly and distinctly, and reinforce that users can re-take the Murphy Method assessment over time to measure improvement and guide next decisions.
